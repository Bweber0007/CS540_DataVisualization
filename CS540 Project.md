**Stakeholders**  
The four datasets used for this project contain historical information about NBA players, teams, standings, and the draft. The analysis of these datasets is tailored to two key groups of stakeholders – NBA executives and general managers and NBA fans. The main subject of this analysis is the NBA draft with a focus on draft pick value and its impact on the team.   
One of the most important aspects of an NBA executive’s career is building a winning team. In order to build a winning team, an executive must make choices that will add value to the team. So, the analysis performed is designed to inform these executives on just how much value they should expect from the specific draft picks they may have accumulated in order to properly build around their current assets. This group of stakeholders is in need of more detailed data-driven analysis that is able to uncover key insights that can support strategic decisions such as the future value versus the current cost of including draft picks in trades, long-term team building decisions, and whether moving up in the draft is worth it.  
The other main group that this analysis is aimed to benefit are NBA fans. This group is not necessarily focused on the nitty-gritty details and minutiae of team building mechanics; they are focused on the big headlines. For fans that support non-playoff teams, one of the most exciting aspects of the fan experience is the NBA draft. This analysis is aimed at providing base-line insights into the historical data and the value that each draft pick provides a team. More importantly, fans want to know if this draft pick will increase their team’s chance of winning. In order to derive these insights, fans do not need low-level details and complex charts; they are focused on the more plain charts that tell them what they want to know.

**Data-Driven Narrative**  
	As we wrap up the 2025-2026 NBA season, one of the hottest topics in the league is tanking. Tanking is when a team purposefully loses in order to give themselves a worse record. Why would any team do this you may ask? The worse a team’s record is, typically, the better the draft pick they get is. This is not a one-to-one relationship since the lottery allows any of the fourteen non-playoff teams to receive a top-four pick. The lottery is performed by drawing numbers and assigning the pick-at-hand to the team whose number was drawn. But, the worse the team is, the more numbers that they are assigned therefore, the better the odds they have at receiving one of these top four lottery picks. After the lottery is decided, teams are assigned the next available pick with priority going to the team with the worst record that hasn’t received a pick yet. So, by losing, teams effectively increase their chance at receiving a better pick.  
	This topic of tanking has permeated every facet of the NBA, including media, fans, players, executives, general managers, and the NBA league office and commissioner. The commissioner has come out and said publicly that they are planning on making changes to combat this tanking epidemic, however, no changes have been made yet. So, NBA executives and general managers have made their decisions already for their teams to tank and NBA fans are anxiously awaiting the final standings and the draft order announcements. While we wait for this year’s results, let’s look into the historical data from the draft and see just what we should expect from each draft pick and what it means for each team.  
	First off, we should look into how a draft pick impacts a team's win percentage for the following years. This chart documents the difference between a team’s win percentage averaged over the next five years after the draft and their win percentage the year leading up to the draft. This difference in win percentage is grouped by pick tiers: 1-3, 4-7, 8-14, 15-20, and 21-30. As you may expect, the better the draft pick, the larger the difference in win percentage. So, on average, the teams that receive these higher draft picks do become better in terms of winning. This is great news for NBA fans that are rooting for their teams to lose in hopes of receiving one of these lottery picks with the expectation of brighter days ahead. But, this conclusion is somewhat obvious and the correlation coefficient r of \-0.57 as displayed in the title is statistically significant, but it is not necessarily a perfect relationship. Furthermore, this grouping has other inherent factors such as the trends amongst the teams that receive these picks. The worst teams in the league have the smallest number of wins, so a small increase in number of wins can result in a larger percentage increase than middle of the pack teams. Also, the teams that receive later picks are the best teams. Since the league is so competitive, it is often hard for these teams to remain the best over a six year span and generally, should expect to win fewer games over time.  
So, since win percentage may not be the best metric for determining how much value a draft pick has, let’s investigate an advanced stat – VORP. Value Over Replacement Player (VORP) is highly related to another advanced stat – Box Plus-Minus (BPM), but BPM is per 100 possessions while VORP is cumulative and is influenced by how many total minutes a player has. So a low-minutes player and a high-minutes player could have comparable BPM, but a large difference in VORP. Furthermore, VORP is relative to a replacement-level player (-2 BPM) which helps to compare historical seasons where play styles have changed since the data is always relative to that season.  
	In order to determine if VORP is a good potential replacement for win percentage, every team’s cumulative VORP was compared to their win percentage. The scatter plot above displays this data grouped by playoff and non-playoff teams. It shows that teams with higher win percentages (playoff teams) generally have a higher total VORP. The correlation coefficient, r, is included in the title and has a value of 0.94. This indicates a near perfect relationship between a team’s total VORP and how well they did that season. Like the previous chart, this provides a somewhat obvious statement: the better the players on a team are, the better the team is going to do. But, we have established that VORP is a good metric for identifying good teams and thus players. It also gives us the ability to better analyze each player individually.  
	The data in this chart shows that the difference between an average non-playoff team and average playoff team is about 10 VORP. This 10 VORP provides a 25% difference in win percentage. It also shows that no team that is able to accumulate 14 total VORP has ever not made the playoffs and after about 10 VORP, a team should expect to win at least 50% of their games.

	Now that we have chosen VORP as our metric, we have concrete numeric goals for determining how a team’s expected VORP translates to success. So, let’s tweak the first chart a bit to investigate how a team’s win percentage is affected by the portion of their total VORP attributed to the players that they have drafted in the first round. Within the chart below, the same color-coded groups, playoff and non-playoff teams, tell us another story. In order to become a playoff-caliber team, the portion of total VORP attributed to their own first-round picks does not matter entirely. There are plenty of teams that make the playoffs which have added no value from the draft. But, like the above chart, teams that can accrue 10 VORP via the draft are near guaranteed to make the playoffs and win at least 50% of their games.

	VORP is a solid metric for determining team success, and attaining it via the draft isn’t the only way that a team can reach the playoffs. But, for small market teams that may not be able to draw high-level talent via free agency or can’t afford to offer large contracts to top free-agents, the best way to accrue this necessary VORP is via the draft. So, how much VORP should fans and executives expect from their draft pick? This box chart above identifies key distribution statistics about each draft pick tier’s career average VORP. It shows that no matter which pick a team may get, there is always a chance that they are a bust and provide no value. It also shows us that picks have generally increasing career average VORP as the pick improves. While almost every pick in the top twenty has the chance of producing similar ceiling superstars, the distribution favors the top picks. Roughly 50% of picks 1-3 provide more VORP than 75% of pick 4-7. This accentuates the importance for the worst teams to jump into the top of the lottery rather than missing out and only getting the fifth or sixth pick. You are twice as likely to achieve similar levels of production from a pick 1-3 than 4-7 not to mention the ceiling difference.  
	All of this discussion of VORP is interesting for the NBA nerds, but how about numbers that we can better understand. This chart above utilizes VORP, but presents it in a new form – as percentiles relative to playoff rotation players. A playoff rotation player was designated as a player that played at least 20 minutes per game for a team that made the playoffs that year. Then all of the first round draft picks were grouped by their overall pick number and the average was calculated for each grouping’s career average VORP. We can then analyze how each overall pick’s average VORP stacks up against this dataset of playoff rotation players. So an average first overall pick’s career average VORP is better than two-thirds (67%) of all playoff team’s rotation players during that playoff season. Since the goal of most executives is to build a playoff-caliber roster, it is important to know how a first round pick can compare to rotation players for a winning team. This chart also provides the percent of players at each pick position that have career averages above 90% of playoff rotation players and as such are classified as stars. For fans, averages, medians, and standard deviations may be interesting to some, but most want to know about the upside, the top-notch players, the homeruns. And for executives who are trying to make long-term plans about which players to build their team around, understanding which picks are capable of providing the backbones for a playoff team are essential. And, with mostly no surprise, the first round pick is the top contributor. The more interesting section is the variance amongst picks 2-5 and the silent assassins in a couple of the late round picks. Picks 2 and 4 both have generally better players, but their star hit rate is comparable to picks 6-16. Picks 3 and 5 combined produce roughly twice as many stars as picks 2 and 4 combined. This is likely attributable to the fact that these top picks are often high upside swings for superstars which can be volatile in terms of attaining superstardom. The other interesting anomaly is that the 30th pick contributes just as many stars as any other pick besides the top 3 contributors and almost no other stars are drafted in the second half of the first round. This could be attributed to the fact that the 30th pick is normally given to one of the top teams in the league and these teams have a winning culture that cultivates good habits which provides the tools needed for these players to become stars.  
	But what about different draft classes? Don’t some years produce more stars or have more solid prospects like this upcoming draft? This bar chart displays the career average VORP and career total VORP for each draft class. As a fan of a team that had a top draft pick or multiple draft picks in a year, but the team didn’t improve as much as you expected, it may be due to that draft class being weak in comparison to other years. This chart by itself may not provide much value for NBA executives in terms of determining future pick value, but it does provide some insights and fuel for creating hypotheses about the league and VORP. Some sidenotes about this chart are that the number of first round draft picks has increased with expansion so the all-time top draft of 1984 only had 24 players and every draft after 2005 has 30 players. This makes the fact that 1984 has the highest career total even more impressive considering that it had 80% of the players in modern drafts. When analyzing the difference between modern era players and older generations we notice that the modern era has generally not produced as many superstar drafts as this chart would suggest the older generations did. Part of this could be attributed to more recent drafts having more active players which means their career averages and total still may be affected and so it is not a truly fair comparison. Also, this difference in draft class size adds six more players to the back of the draft, which likely decreases career average numbers since these players are usually not as good as the top 24 players. In the interactive version of this graph, the hover labels for each draft class also include the average rookie age. The 1984 draft had an average rookie age of 22.3 and the 2018 draft had an average rookie age of 20.3. This difference is present in all modern and older drafts. This two year difference could also be a contributing factor to the differences in VORP. Most older generations went to college for a full four years before going into the draft while today's generation prioritizes getting top talent in more quickly.  
	Now we know that different draft classes definitely have different levels of player value in terms of career VORP. So, how does that information impact what we should expect from the draft? The strip plot above contains each player’s career average VORP grouped at the pick they were drafted at, but with their values standardized to their draft class and represented as Z-scores. The average of these Z-scores is then calculated for each pick. So this chart shows how much better or worse each player and the average player from that pick is relative to the other players picked in their respective draft. The y-axis Z-score values represent how many standard deviations away from the mean a player is. So players and picks around 0 provide average career VORP compared to how much VORP the rest of that draft class produced. The line is the linear interpolation of these values, which can loosely be translated to how much value that pick should provide relative to the value that all other picks provide. The key insights derived from this graph validate some of those that were analyzed in other graphs. The first half of the draft, picks 1-15, have a higher ceiling than the back half. Picks 1 and 3 produce the best players on average, picks 2, 4, and 5 produce better players on average, and all other picks produce average players on average. When considering pick position and the linear trend line, picks 1 and 3 produce the most value relative to the other picks, and even though it only has an average slightly above zero, the 30th pick provides much more value than it should if a linear decrease in value should be expected as the draft progresses.  
**Visualization Effectiveness**  
	These visualizations are effective at communicating the insights derived from the data by focusing on the overarching goals and questions that needed answering. NBA executives need to know the value of a draft pick and how each pick can contribute to their long-term planning goals. NBA fans want to know if and how much their team's future winning increased from tanking. The first scatter plot provides a basic graph for fans to understand how much their future winning is affected by attaining a specific pick. The second and third scatter plots provide NBA executives information about how VORP can be used as a metric to determine team success and the thresholds for winning. The box plot is simple enough for fans to understand it and provides general analysis of picks by tier for executives. The first bar chart combines a simple display with powerful insights. Its simple format contains easy to understand information about which draft picks provide a path to a superstar, which is a huge draw for fans and also provide executives an idea of how they can build their team long-term. The second bar chart is a fun and interesting chart for fans to compare draft classes through the years; entertainment is an important aspect of fandom. Finally, the last scatter plot combines multiple aspects from the previous charts to show just how much value a pick has on average in comparison to the rest of the draft class. This can be crucial information for an executive when determining which future draft assets and picks may provide the most ROI when determining whether or not to include picks or which picks to acquire in trades.  
**Customization Justification**  
	Almost every chart follows the same red-blue color palette which increases the readability of them as a combined analysis by providing a uniform display. The inclusion of the trend lines in certain charts help to easily identify the patterns in the data without having to overanalyze them. The inclusion of mean markers in the scatter plots also help readers to easily identify key trends and how data is distributed without having to overanalyze them. The use of red for both the trends and red and black mean markers amplifies this focus on key data points so that users can easily identify them without dealing with a cluttered visual. The stacked bar chart allows for multi-level analysis within the same chart and same x-axis position without overcrowding the chart. The draft strength bar chart uses color to embrace multi-level analysis in order to easily identify the best and worst groups. The simple plotly\_white chart backgrounds and light blue grid lines provide a simple and pleasing environment for the data to live without cluttering the visual while still providing needed structure and context. The axis labels, legends, and titles are all simplistic in font, font size, font color, and placement. This uniformity helps to provide a clean base for readers to know where to look for information especially when viewing multiple charts.
